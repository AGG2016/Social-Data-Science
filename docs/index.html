What this is

A portal aimed to provide social scientists with explanations on basic statistics and the application of statistical tools to social research, based on examples, case studies and a library of R codes focused mainly in the visual exploration of data.

What this is not

The very precise goal of providing social scientists with examples of quantitative methods applied to the exploration of social phenomena is best served by a humble attitude, and the recognition of the scope and limitations of this contribution. This website does not pretend to be:

  • A textbook on statistics

  • A programming manual

  • A comprehensive research methods manual

Inception: an idea stuck in my mind

This site was born almost accidentally, as part of an effort to teach social sciences students basic statistical concepts, alongside with research applications of these concepts using R, with a special focus on web publishing and reproducible research, which nowadays constitute one of the distinctive features of Data Science.

It started as a plain collection of very basic web pages, aimed to show my students both examples of visualizations and model outputs, and the interpretation of them in an agile, flexible and easy to share format.

As I worked on simple R Markdown documents, I inadvertently embarked myself in the adventure of putting together a sort of statistics handbook for social scientists, gathering a selection of examples of application of statistical models and analyzes to social topics, central definitions of the statistical inference and R coding for their implementation.

About me

My years of experience in business analytics, in which I have worked particularly hard creating visualizations and narratives intended to present statistical results, in a meaningful, persuasive, yet technically correct way, often having in front an audience with no statistical background, have become handy in order to find resourceful explanations of deep and important concepts, regularly overlooked or misunderstood by scientists which in general will not focus on quantitative analysis.

Also, my experience as undergraduate statistics instructor has shown me there is -still- a sense of schism between quantitative and qualitative approaches to complex social phenomena and therefore there is plenty of room for pedagogical contributions aiming to surpass this seeming chasm, this apparently irreconciliable relationship between numbers and social theory.
